## 1. Lead Answer

TAP should be framed as a lean, system-level portfolio and execution mechanism that attaches senior UN leadership decisions to distributed entity-level delivery capacity.

TAP should not duplicate existing innovation hubs. The 23 June the UNIN interviewee and WP15 interview (internal) materials sharpen that answer:

- UN innovation capacity is real but distributed. WFP, UNICEF, UNDP, UNHCR, ITU, Global Pulse, DPO and others hold pieces of implementation capacity.
- System-level exchange exists, but system-level execution is weak. UNIN connects and curates; DTN coordinates technology actors; UN 2.0/Global Pulse provide capability and accelerator functions. None alone provides a durable, funded, senior-leadership-to-delivery mechanism.
- The design gap is not another hub. The gap is a repeatable way to identify system-level priorities, attach funding/governance, assemble surge teams from existing entity capacity, and transition solutions into long-term owners.
- The funding model is the "teeth". Without flexible pooled funding or committed entity contributions, TAP risks becoming another coordination layer.

## 4. Updated Research Answers

### RQ-001: Who are the key actors and stakeholders in the UN innovation ecosystem?

Answer: The ecosystem should be mapped by function, not by logo. The useful categories are:

- Entity-level implementation capacity: WFP, UNICEF, UNDP, UNHCR, ITU, DPO and other entity innovation/digital/data teams.
- System-level exchange and knowledge: UNIN, UN 2.0 communities of practice, capacity-building/training actors.
- System-level coordination/decision channels: DTN, HLCM/CEB-related mechanisms, UN80 Steering Committee while active.
- Flexible system-level delivery enablers: Global Pulse, UNICC, Digital ID/Data Commons coalitions, pooled funds.
- Adoption owners: programme, country, shared-service, or business owners who can commit to transition and reuse.

Evidence: WP15 interview (internal); WP15 interview (internal); innovation-jobs dataset; TAP working draft (internal).

### RQ-002: How is innovation currently delivered across entity and cross-entity levels?

Answer: Innovation is delivered through a mixed architecture: entity accelerators and labs, digital/data teams, programme-led pilots, funds, communities of practice, and ad hoc inter-agency coalitions. No single UN innovation model dominates. This supports TAP as an orchestrator and execution-enabler, not a centralized replacement.

Evidence: WFP and UNICEF patterns in the jobs dataset; the UNIN interviewee's comment that models are similar but not codified; UNGP Scaling the Summit; WP15 interview (internal).

### RQ-003: What processes, roles, and barriers characterize the ecosystem?

Answer: The process usually moves from idea/demand signal -> pilot/prototype -> internal validation -> attempted scale. Support breaks at ownership, technical delivery, flexible funding, procurement/legal, cross-entity resource sharing, and adoption/transition. Funding often appears as the named problem but is frequently a proxy for missing execution resources.

Evidence: WP15 interview (internal); UNGP scaling report; TAP Business Needs Survey slide; WP15 interview (internal).

### RQ-004: Where is the ecosystem strongest, and where are the gaps?

Answer: The ecosystem is strongest at entity-level innovation and distributed technical/programmatic expertise. The critical gap is system-level implementation capacity: a mechanism that lets senior leadership decide priorities and then mobilize entity-level capacity for system-level use cases.

Evidence: WP15 interview (internal) on Data Commons and entity-level teams; WP15 interview (internal) on UNIN not being an implementer; 2024 UN 2.0 Assessment slide in working draft.

### RQ-005: How do innovations travel through hubs, and where does support break?

Answer: Grassroots ideas and entity innovations can move through WFP, Global Pulse, UNICEF, UNIN and other channels, but they often do not cross the chasm into multi-entity adoption. Support breaks when there is no mandate, no common owner, no pooled funding, no technical "doing" team, or no sustainable service-provider/cost-recovery route.

Evidence: WP15 interview (internal) on Global Pulse cohorts and Data Commons; WP15 interview (internal) on Digital ID and technical execution; UNGP scaling journey.

### RQ-006: What operating models exist, and what do they teach TAP?

Answer: TAP should learn from at least five models:

- WFP Innovation Accelerator: structured path to scale, funding, mentorship, hands-on support, operational access.
- UNICEF Venture Fund: staged non-dilutive funding, open-source requirement, measurable real-time data, growth-stage funding.
- Global Pulse accelerator: system-wide cohort/support mechanism and flexible innovation capacity.
- Digital ID/Data Commons: small technical ownership plus cross-entity leadership mandate.
- CRAF'd/MPTF model: pooled financing, steering committee, secretariat, administrative agent, results reporting.

Evidence: Local interviews and working draft; WFP Innovation page; UNICEF Venture Fund page; CRAF'd MPTF page.

### RQ-007: What external accelerator best practices should be adapted?

Answer: The useful practices are not cosmetic accelerator rituals. TAP should adapt:

- Stage gates and stop/go decisions.
- Fast sprints for uncertainty reduction.
- Adoption-owner checks before scale funding.
- Structured technical support and partner bench.
- Portfolio-level measurement, not idea-count measurement.
- Clear transition route into operations, cost recovery, or service provider ownership.

Evidence: Executive deck; working draft external benchmark slide; OECD/OPSI and NHS-style adoption logic; WFP/UNICEF examples.

### RQ-008: What criteria define TAP-worthy digital/technology innovation?

Answer: A TAP-worthy use case should clear four filters:

- New or newly adapted to context: not routine ICT maintenance.
- Technology-enabled: digital, data, AI, platform, emerging technology, or shared technology component.
- System-wide potential: value crosses entities, countries, or shared functions.
- Adoption pathway: named problem/adoption owner, transition route, and measurable benefit.

Explicit exclusions: generic helpdesk work, routine data entry, ordinary communications, and one-entity IT maintenance.

Evidence: Executive deck RQ-004; working draft slide on definition; UN80 report paragraphs 76-79.

### RQ-009: What pain points block delivery and scale?

Answer: The main blockers are fragmentation, funding constraints, policy/legal/governance constraints, limited technical capacity, weak adoption ownership, earmarked funding, time scarcity, and culture/leadership gaps. The TAP Business Needs Survey already supports these categories, but the raw survey still needs validation.

Evidence: TAP survey slide in working draft; WP15 interview (internal); UNGP scaling report.

### RQ-010: What support is needed at different maturity levels?

Answer: Support should change by maturity:

- Discovery/demand: use surveys, communities, accelerator pipelines, country/team pain points, and horizon scanning.
- Seed/pilot: provide sprint funding, technical assessment, project management, data/security/legal checks.
- Implementation: assemble surge task force, secure owner commitments, de-block procurement/legal/governance.
- Scale: fund adoption and interoperability work; connect service providers and country/entity adopters.
- Transition: hand over to shared service provider, implementing entity, cost-recovery mechanism, or pooled fund window.

Evidence: Working draft portfolio cycle; WP15 interview (internal); UNICEF Venture Fund stage logic; WFP path-to-scale logic.

### RQ-011: What should inform demand signals, prioritization, and metrics?

Answer: Demand should not rely only on a Microsoft Forms-style survey. TAP should triangulate:

- Business needs survey results.
- Existing accelerator/case pipelines.
- Official innovation/digital job postings and capability signals.
- Pooled funding and donor-priority signals.
- CIO/DTN/UNIN/UN 2.0 community inputs.
- Existing use-case progress data from Data Commons, Beneficiary ID, Translation, and Expertise-on-Demand.

Prioritization should score system value, urgency, adoption readiness, reuse potential, technical feasibility, risk, and availability of owners.

Evidence: WP15 interview (internal) on WFP demand signal being structurally smarter; jobs dataset; TAP survey; working draft.

### RQ-012: Who is TAP's client?

Answer: TAP's client is the accountable problem/adoption owner, not simply the innovator. Innovators are partners. The client must be able to commit to adoption, funding or cost recovery, legal/policy path, and transition. For system-level assets, this may be a coalition or shared-service owner.

Evidence: Executive deck RQ-011; WP15 interview (internal) on business ownership; WP15 interview (internal) on leadership-to-ground link.

### RQ-013: What operating, governance, and funding model should TAP use?

Answer: Use a hybrid model:

- TAP Coordination Unit: lean, execution-focused, prepares portfolio, tracks delivery, supports fundraising, maintains evidence.
- Surge Task Forces: time-bound teams around use cases, drawing entity experts, technical capacity, project management, vendors/partners.
- UN Innovation Ecosystem: source of knowledge, demand, solutions, owners and experts.
- Steering Committee: quarterly approval of portfolio, funding envelope, stage decisions, and escalation.
- Pooled funding: flexible fund or joint programme structure with clear safeguards; case-by-case fundraising should be avoided as the default because it is too slow and donor-driven.

Governance must separate problem selection, technical validation, funding allocation, delivery oversight, and adoption commitment.

Evidence: TAP working draft (internal) slides 22-28; WP15 interview (internal) on pooled funding and senior leadership; CRAF'd governance model.

### RQ-014: What investment posture and KPIs should TAP use?

Answer: TAP should use a catalytic, stage-based public-value venture posture:

- Seed/sprint: small, fast, flexible tickets for learning and feasibility.
- Scale-up: larger targeted funding after evidence and owner commitment.
- Transition: sustainable route through service provider, cost recovery, pooled fund, or entity handover.

KPIs should track portfolio flow and adoption, not idea volume:

- Number of seed and scale-up use cases.
- Time from demand signal to funded setup.
- Number of entities contributing to surge task forces.
- Named adoption owners and completed transitions.
- Reuse across entities/country teams.
- Value delivered vs. business case.
- Integrity/risk indicators: cybersecurity, data protection, ethics, procurement/legal readiness.

Evidence: Executive deck RQ-013/RQ-014; working draft KPI slide; UNICEF/WFP examples.

### RQ-015: What pathway and risks should guide TAP setup?

Answer: TAP should move from UN80 temporary governance into a standing joint programme or MPTF-style mechanism:

- Mandate and sponsor route.
- Demand and portfolio thesis.
- First proposals and owner commitments.
- Delivery through surge teams and pooled capacity.
- Transition to sustainable owners.
- Institutionalize as a standing UN 2.0 acceleration capability.

Key risks:

- Coordination-only failure: no technical team, no authority, no money.
- Duplication risk: appearing to compete with WFP, UNICEF, Global Pulse, UNIN, DTN or UNICC.
- Funding capture: donor preferences distort portfolio selection.
- Ownership failure: no adoption owner or transition route.
- Evidence weakness: WIP survey/deck figures cited as final without raw validation.
- Political/institutional risk: post-UN80 governance route unclear.
- Security/legal risk: AI/data/shared platform use cases need early risk gating.

Evidence: UN80 report paragraphs 76-79; WP15 interview (internal); WP15 interview (internal); working draft; UNGP scaling report.

## 6. Cloud-Agent Secondary Data Loop

Goal: Set up a cloud-agent loop that continuously substantiates TAP claims with real-world secondary data and produces a claim-to-evidence ledger for the deck.

### Folder/Data Model

Recommended local structure:

- data/secondary-evidence/source_registry.yml
- data/secondary-evidence/claim_ledger.jsonl
- data/secondary-evidence/actor_map.csv
- data/secondary-evidence/jobs_snapshot_YYYYMMDD.json
- data/secondary-evidence/funding_snapshot_YYYYMMDD.csv
- data/secondary-evidence/case_studies_YYYYMMDD.jsonl
- outputs/tap_research_brief_YYYYMMDD.md
- outputs/tap_deck_claim_updates_YYYYMMDD.md

### Agent Loop

1. **Source Watcher Agent** — Watches local TAP folder, UN80 documents, MPTF Gateway, IATI API Gateway, WFP Innovation, UNICEF Venture Fund, Global Pulse, OECD OPSI, CEB/HLCM/DTN pages if available. Output: changed-source registry with URL/path, date, owner, access status, and evidence type.
2. **Document Extractor Agent** — Converts PPTX, PDF, DOCX, XLSX, HTML into text. Output: text extracts, metadata, keyword snippets, duplicate detection.
3. **Innovation Jobs Agent** — Re-runs and extends innovation-jobs/collect_innovation_jobs.py. Adds WHO/FAO/Taleo, UNOPS certificate handling, Inspira/UN Secretariat, UNFPA, UNOPS, UNICC if accessible. Output: monthly distribution of innovation/digital/AI/data/programme roles by agency, source type, role family, and geography.
4. **Funding and Governance Agent** — Pulls MPTF Gateway fund/project/contribution data and IATI activities. Tags pooled funds by governance model, contributors, administrative agent, participating organizations, disbursement pattern, and digital/data/innovation relevance. Output: evidence for "pooled funding is feasible", "which governance models already exist", and "which donors support system-level digital/data work".
5. **Case Study Agent** — Tracks WFP Accelerator, UNICEF Venture Fund, Global Pulse accelerator, CRAF'd, Digital ID/Data Commons, NHS Innovation Accelerator, OECD OPSI cases. Extracts stage gates, support package, funding ticket size, adoption owner, scale metrics, and transition model. Output: benchmark matrix for RQ-006/RQ-007/RQ-010/RQ-014.
6. **Demand Signal Agent** — Compares TAP survey results, WFP/Global Pulse accelerator pipelines, UNIN/UN 2.0 community signals, jobs data, and funding flows. Output: top recurring demand themes and which are survey-only vs. validated by external secondary data.
7. **Claim Auditor Agent** — Maintains claim_ledger.jsonl. Each claim has: claim text, source IDs, evidence type, confidence, validation status, stale date, deck slide, and next check. Output: "safe to cite", "cite with caveat", "do not cite yet".
8. **Synthesis/Deck Agent** — Produces slide-ready bullets and a one-page weekly research delta. It must not invent final claims; it can only promote claims from "needs_review" to "working_confident" when the Claim Auditor has enough evidence.

### Weekly Cadence

- Monday AM Geneva: run watchers and extraction.
- Monday midday: refresh jobs/funding/case data.
- Tuesday: claim auditor updates evidence statuses.
- Wednesday: synthesis agent prepares deck deltas and interview-question updates.
- Friday: human review with Rahul/working team; promote only reviewed claims.
